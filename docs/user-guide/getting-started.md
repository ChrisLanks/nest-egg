# Getting Started with Nest Egg

Welcome! This guide is for people who are new to personal finance tracking —
no jargon, no assumptions, just plain English.

---

## What is Nest Egg?

Nest Egg is a private, household financial dashboard. It pulls together all
your bank accounts, credit cards, investments, and loans in one place so you
can answer questions like:

- Where is my money going every month?
- Am I saving enough for retirement?
- What is my net worth right now — and is it growing?
- Can my partner and I retire at the same time?

You don't need to be a finance expert to use it. Most people get useful
information within 10 minutes of signing up.

---

## Step 1 — Create your account

Go to the app and click **Sign Up**. You'll need:

- An email address
- A password (at least 12 characters — the app will guide you)

After you register, you'll get a verification email. Click the link in that
email to confirm your address. (Check your spam folder if you don't see it
within a minute.)

---

## Step 2 — Tell us what you want to work on

After you verify your email, Nest Egg will ask you one question:
**"What brings you here?"**

Pick the goal that fits best:

| Goal | What it means |
|------|---------------|
| **Track my spending** | You want to know where your money goes and set a budget |
| **Plan for retirement** | You want to know if you'll have enough money to stop working when you want |
| **Understand my investments** | You have a 401(k) or brokerage account and want to see what's in it and what it's costing you |

Don't stress about picking the "right" one — you can explore everything once
you're set up.

---

## Step 3 — Connect a bank account

This is the fastest way to get useful information. When you connect a bank
account, Nest Egg automatically imports your transactions and tracks your
balances.

**Is this safe?**
Yes. Nest Egg connects through a service called **Plaid**, which is the same
technology used by apps like Venmo, Robinhood, and Coinbase. When you connect,
you log in directly on your bank's own secure page — Nest Egg never sees your
password. The connection is read-only: we can see your transactions and
balances, but we cannot move money.

We support over 11,000 banks and credit unions including Chase, Bank of
America, Wells Fargo, Citi, Capital One, and most regional banks and credit
unions.

**What if I don't want to connect a bank?**
That's fine. You can skip this step and add accounts manually — just enter
your balance by hand and update it whenever you like. You won't get automatic
transaction imports, but you can still track net worth and set goals.

---

## Step 4 — Set up your household

If you share finances with a partner, you can invite them to join your
household. Each person keeps their own login and you can control exactly what
they can see and do. Your combined accounts, budgets, and net worth appear
together in one view.

You can invite your partner now during setup, or come back to it later from
**Settings → Household**.

---

## Step 5 — Pick your dashboard style

Nest Egg will ask how much you want to see when you first open the app:

- **Keep it simple** — Just the essentials: net worth, recent spending, and a
  checklist to guide your next steps. Recommended if you're new.
- **Show me everything** — Every chart and panel from day one. Good if you've
  used finance apps before and want to dive in.

You can always add or remove panels later by clicking **Customize** on the
dashboard.

## Your first week

Once you're set up, here are four things worth doing in your first week:

### 1. Connect a bank account (if you haven't yet)
Everything in the app gets better with real data. Transactions, budgets, and
net worth all pull from your accounts automatically.

### 2. Set one budget
Pick a spending category you already know is a problem — maybe Dining Out,
Groceries, or Shopping — and set a monthly dollar limit. Nest Egg will show
you how you're tracking in real time.

**What's a budget category?**
When you spend money, each transaction gets a category (like "Groceries" or
"Gas"). A budget is just a monthly limit on one of those categories.
Example: "I want to spend no more than $400 on groceries this month."

### 3. Create a savings goal
Name something you're saving toward — an emergency fund, a vacation, a down
payment — and set a target amount. Nest Egg tracks how close you are.

**A note on emergency funds**: Financial advisors generally recommend having
3–6 months of living expenses saved somewhere accessible (like a savings
account). If you don't have this yet, it's a great first goal to set.

### 4. Check your net worth
Your net worth = everything you own minus everything you owe.

Example:
- Checking account: $2,500
- Savings account: $8,000
- Car loan balance: −$6,000
- Credit card balance: −$1,200
- **Net worth: $3,300**

It doesn't matter if your net worth is low or even negative right now — what
matters is whether it's trending in the right direction over time. Nest Egg
plots this on a chart so you can see your progress month by month.

## Glossary — plain English definitions

| Term | What it means |
|------|---------------|
| **Net worth** | Everything you own minus everything you owe. The single most useful number to track over time. |
| **Budget** | A monthly spending limit for one category (e.g., $300 for Groceries). |
| **Transaction** | A single purchase, payment, or deposit. |
| **Category** | The type of spending a transaction belongs to (Groceries, Dining, Utilities, etc.). |
| **Savings goal** | A target amount you're working toward (e.g., $10,000 emergency fund). |
| **Retirement account** | A special account (like a 401k or IRA) where your money grows tax-advantaged until you retire. |
| **401(k)** | A retirement savings account offered by many employers. Your contributions are often matched by your employer — free money. |
| **IRA** | An Individual Retirement Account you open yourself, separate from an employer. |
| **Portfolio** | All your investments together — stocks, funds, etc. |
| **Cash flow** | The difference between money coming in (income) and money going out (spending) each month. |
| **FIRE** | Financial Independence, Retire Early — a goal of saving enough that you no longer need to work. |
| **Tax lot** | A record of when you bought a specific investment, used for calculating gains and taxes when you sell. |
| **Cost basis** | What you originally paid for an investment — used to calculate your gain or loss when you sell. |
| **Net operating income** | For rental properties: rent collected minus expenses (repairs, insurance, etc.). |

## Common questions

**Do I have to connect a bank account?**
No. You can use Nest Egg entirely with manually entered accounts. You'll miss
automatic transaction imports but can still track net worth, budgets, and goals.

**Is my financial data private?**
Yes. Your data is only visible to you and people you explicitly invite to your
household. It is never sold or shared with third parties.

**Can I use Nest Egg with my partner?**
Yes — this is one of its best features. Invite your partner from
**Settings → Household**. You each keep separate logins and can see a combined
view of your finances, including combined net worth and shared budgets.

**I connected my bank but I don't see all my transactions. Why?**
Most bank connections import the last 90 days of transactions by default.
Transactions from before that window won't appear. New transactions should
sync within a few hours.

**What if I disconnect a bank account?**
Disconnecting stops new transactions from syncing, but your existing
transaction history remains in the app. You can reconnect at any time.

**I feel overwhelmed by all the features. Where do I start?**
Start with just three things: connect one account, set one budget, and check
your net worth page. The rest of the app is there when you're ready for it.
The Getting Started checklist on your dashboard will guide you.
